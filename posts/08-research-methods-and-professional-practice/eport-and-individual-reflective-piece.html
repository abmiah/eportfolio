Research Methods and Professional Practice – Unit 12

Friday, 18 April 2025, 22:38 AM

Part One: e-Portfolio artefacts review

1. Introduction

This module, Research Methods and Professional Practice, led by Karen Outram, consists of 12 units. Unlike previous modules that exclusively focused on specific study programs, such as MSc Cyber Security, this module welcomes students from various disciplines within the computing department. This interdisciplinary makeup has encouraged valuable insights into diverse cognitive approaches and methodologies among the student cohort.

The module included three primary assignments, comprising this reflective piece and several formative activities that students were required to complete. In addition to these formative exercises, students were expected to participate in two collaborative discussions, which included a minimum of three peer responses and a summary from one initial post.

This module greatly emphasised the importance of e-portfolios. For each assignment, students must update their e-portfolios to accurately reflect their course progress, outlining any learning.

Given the constraints of this word count, one can only briefly describe only one unit artefact activity, which will include a links to my e-portfolio.

2. Unit 1 – Collaborative Discussion 1

The first discussion, lasting three weeks, focused on a case study of Malware Disruption, analysing the ethical implications of Rogue Web Services. This hosting provider attracts clients who exploit its services for harmful purposes, such as distributing malware and spam. Their slogan reveals a troubling disregard for responsibility, avoiding interaction with ISPs despite being aware of illicit activities. Rogue Web Services breaches ethical standards concerning harm avoidance and public security. Jurisdictional differences complicate the fight against cybercrime. Organisations like these prioritise profit over ethics, fully aware of their clients' harmful activities.

Link to post: Initial Post - Case Study on Malware Disruption

Another formative activity that one had to cover was Ethics in Computing, which reviewed Corrêa et al. (2023), analysing 200 AI ethics guidelines to establish a consensus on ethical principles for AI. Findings indicate a significant rise in AI investments, with U.S. firms receiving $90 billion in 2021. The study outlines 17 key ethical principles, such as accountability, human rights, and privacy. The authors stress the need for transparent regulatory frameworks and diverse perspectives in ethical discussions, noting limitations in linguistic scope and regional representation. Recent developments, including the EU’s AI Act, aim to regulate AI and address ethical risks. The EU Act's AI Opportunities Action Plan does not apply to the UK. The central theme highlights the importance of managing AI ethics for a positive societal impact.

Link: Ethics in Computing in the age of Generative AI

3. Unit 2 - e-Portfolio Activity: Literature Review and Research Proposal Outline

Utilising the template derived from the “Literature Review Guide Questions,” which includes nine inquiries designed to assist students in conducting their literature review, this resource systematically addresses the identification of the target audience, clarifies the significance of the evaluation, and outlines the expected content along with other crucial components. The primary objective is to streamline the literature review writing process for scholars. The guiding questions promote an unbiased approach for students undertaking a literature review, thereby enhancing objectivity in their analysis. This methodology encourages students to evaluate any discrepancies within the available research sources and recognise the limitations present in those studies.

Link: Writing a Literature Review

4. Unit 3 - e-Portfolio Activity: Research Proposal Activity

This activity presents three questions for our research proposal: selecting a suitable methodology, identifying data collection methods, and outlining essential skills for successful project execution.

My chosen literature review adopted a deductive quantitative methodology to investigate the “Financial Impact of Ransomware and Malware in the Health Sector in the UK” . It examines the financial repercussions of cyberattacks within the UK healthcare system, primarily utilising secondary data sources that reflect losses incurred, particularly concerning the NHS. The data sources will be carefully chosen from government publications, ensuring accuracy and reliability. The acquired data should assess how the appropriate measures were implemented to mitigate potential future exposure.

Link: Research Proposal Review

5. Unit 4 - Literature Review Outline Submission

My outline was structured into three basic sections: an introduction, a research question, and a conclusion.

The research will critically examine the historical impact of malware on operations within the NHS, with a particular emphasis on the financial ramifications experienced by the NHS and private sectors. Employing a deductive approach, the study evaluates trends in healthcare cyber threats in the UK, utilising credible data sources. Notable cyberattacks are identified, and mitigation strategies are assessed, comparing the responses of the NHS to those of the private sector. Limitations of the study include a reliance on the availability of existing reports and inherent challenges in quantifying the financial impacts of cyber incidents. The conclusion will likely highlight the necessity for independent analyses of cyberattacks, underscore the significance of lessons learned, and advocate for further research to pre-empt future incidents.

Link: Literature Review Project Outline

6. Unit 5 - Reflective Activity 2: Case Study: Inappropriate Use of Survey

The case of Cambridge Analytica, a UK data consultancy scrutinised for collecting data from over 87 million Facebook users without consent through an app that misled users about its intended purpose, raised significant ethical, legal, and social issues regarding privacy violations and breaches of the Data Protection Act 1998. This data was used for psychographic profiling and targeted advertising, notably in the 2016 Trump and Brexit campaigns. The ICO raided Cambridge Analytica, seizing records and imposing penalties for data breaches. Facebook faced backlash and agreed to a $725 million settlement concerning the scandal, highlighting concerns about data privacy and corporate responsibility in protecting user information.

Link: Case Study: Inappropriate Use of Surveys

Part Two: Reflection

11. Introduction

Unlike other modules that focused exclusively on our core study concepts, particularly MSc Cyber Security in my case, I must admit that this module felt especially challenging and, in several ways, somewhat disorganised. This will be further discussed in the worksheet section. The module required not only the primary assignment, which is graded and includes a literature review, research presentation, and the current module's e-portfolio and reflection (this paper), but also several formative activities that, although not graded, were mandatory to support students in writing this paper. Managing all these assignments was occasionally challenging; what initially seemed like a part-time course has evolved into a full-time commitment. Balancing a full-time job and family responsibilities while striving to excel in all assignments became a difficult juggling act, as the course material was rich with valuable information and informative articles designed to enhance students' understanding of the module.

In this reflection section, I will focus exclusively on the areas where I faced greater challenges compared to other units and those that provided valuable insights for my continued growth. Additionally, I will discuss my SWOT analysis and future action plan to enhance my development in cybersecurity.

12. Worksheet

I do not consider myself proficient in Excel and have only used the program for basic calculations and creating simple spreadsheets rather than for any advanced statistical analysis. However, students were required to complete several mandatory statistical worksheets; conversely, there was confusion about which exercise file to open on the hypothesis testing sheet. The document instructs us to open the file “Exa 8.4F.xlsx”; however, no file of that name exists. Instead, the file “Exa 7.4F.xlsx” was available on the worksheet download. I can only presume this was an oversight by the course administrators; Several of my peers expressed their confusion, which was frustrating since the formative aspect was a requirement that one would think would have been structured accordingly. It was also noted that several worksheets were missing, forcing us to move on to the next one.

Another source of frustration was that, although it was not the course administrators' fault, the examples were shown using Excel, which was catered for Windows computers. Since I am using an Apple Mac, many instructions varied from the functions needed on Macs, requiring me to look up the Mac equivalents for Excel.

Some concepts listed on the worksheet were not fully explained. For example, on the histogram worksheet, the upper-class boundaries (UCB) range was not adequately detailed, nor was the application of those sets of numbers. Additionally, the concept of “=TTEST” was not fully explained.

What?
This module has encountered various challenges, primarily due to unclear guidance within the units. The statistical component, essential for understanding, was disorganised, with some incomplete sections. Despite these issues, learning new deductive and inductive research methodology concepts has provided a strong foundation for future studies.

So What?
Several academic researchers have used statistical analysis, especially the TTEST function in Excel, to compare data. I understand the benefits of fully grasping how to use this effectively in research but cramming it all into 12 weeks alongside the primary assignment would not be feasible. To enhance personal growth and professional academic research, one needs to explore this feature independently.

Now What?
Based on my experience in statistical analysis, I believe that exploring a course in data analysis, both in Excel and Python, along with expanding one’s skill set in SQL, will enhance one’s knowledge and ability to utilise data management to advance one's career fully. I am not suggesting that one pursue a degree or master's course in data analysis, but rather that having a solid foundational working knowledge is useful within cybersecurity.

15. Conclusion

The outlook for cybersecurity professionals remains uncertain, as there have been many discussions about the threat of AI taking over various industries. For example, in web development, code can be generated with just a few lines of commands, and within seconds, one can create a functional website. Additionally, Trump's tariff policies and the ongoing conflicts in Gaza and Ukraine contribute to significant uncertainty and ethical concerns globally.

However, despite the gloomy perspective on global political issues, one must remain optimistic about the job market for cybersecurity professionals, as the threat landscape for cybercriminals will not cease; in fact, one could argue that threat actors might take advantage of and increase their stakes in unstable industries. According to Fleck (2024), global cybercrime is set to grow substantially, and predictions for 2028 suggest it could cost the global economy $13.82 trillion.

As our daily lives become heavily reliant on technology, IoT devices, and digital platforms such as social media or e-commerce sites, the need for security measures to protect the general public from data theft and industries from losing significant amounts of money due to ransomware or data breaches must be addressed. Therefore, cybersecurity professionals should be considered an essential requirement for all sectors.

In my original post, I discuss the significance of ethical responsibility across diverse professional domains, emphasising the imperative of adhering to legal frameworks and ethical standards to mitigate adverse social ramifications and sustain public trust. It is incumbent upon professionals to prioritise public safety by effectively communicating any potential risks linked to the data, even when such communication requires additional analytical efforts to facilitate a thorough comprehension of the circumstances.

Busilas (2025) emphasises the crucial role of ethical responsibilities in research, noting that the obligations associated with Abi's tasks extend beyond mere data analysis to encompass the responsibility of ensuring that the data is accurate and free from bias. This perspective is corroborated by the Association for Computing Machinery (2018), which asserts that researchers should minimise the potential for harm to others and adhere to established best practices.

Kosar (2025) points out that if Abi's findings suggest a potential health risk based on his data, it would be unethical to obscure this information. This perspective aligns with the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics (2018), which asserts that when delivering nutritional services, the primary focus should remain on the customer.

According to Diekman et al. (2022), argues that nutritional practitioners must present evidence in a critically sound and accurate manner. This notion was emphasised by Younes (2025), who underscores the ethical practices necessary for conveying reliable data.

Summary - Case Study on Malware Disruption

Collaborative Learning Discussion - Unit 3

Monday, 10 February 2025, 11:33 PM

In my initial post, I conducted an analysis of a case study concerning Malware Disruption, as delineated in the ACM Code of Ethics and Professional Conduct (Association for Computing Machinery, 2024). This study investigates the ethical dilemmas associated with a company named Rogue Web Services and compares those ethical considerations with the standards outlined by the British Computer Society (BCS, 2022). Rogue Web Services markets itself with the slogan, "Cheap, guaranteed uptime, no matter what," which has resulted in attracting clients who utilise its services to host websites laden with malware and facilitate the distribution of spam. Furthermore, Rogue Web Services has demonstrated an unwillingness to engage with other Internet Service Providers (ISPs) that have expressed concerns regarding their practices, indicating a troubling lack of accountability and adherence to ethical standards; furthermore, addressing cybercrime presents considerable challenges owing to the conflicting jurisdictional regulations and the intricacies of national legislation.

The analysis presented in this discourse critically examines the ethical frameworks delineated by the Association for Computing Machinery (2018) and the British Computer Society (BCS, 2022). Both ethical frameworks emphasise the paramount importance of public health, security, and the adherence to ethical standards within the computing profession. Numerous peers have suggested areas for further exploration within my initial post. Osman (2025) highlighted the deficiency of due diligence regarding the company’s inadequate engagement with other ISP entities. Meanwhile, Kosar (2025) identified ethical dilemmas arising from varying jurisdictional contexts, exemplified by the case involving encrypted communication services provided by EncroChat. Kosar (2025) further elucidates the complexities associated with national jurisdiction and references the German Federal Supreme Court (BGH), which mandates that domain registrars uphold a duty of care, compelling them to take action upon suspicion of any illicit activities (Nordemann, 2021).

Ziemele (2025) posits that the analysis could have more thoroughly explored the various forms of harm potentially perpetrated by Rogue’s clients and the infringement of GDPR regulations. Furthermore, Younes (2025) contends that adopting ethical obligations to prevent misuse is crucial, given the significant implications of marketing strategies on the landscape of cybercrime.

Exercise 6.1:

In this example, the instructions ask to open the files titled Exa8.1B.xlsx, which contain the data needed to run the test example.

The instruction document states that one should work out the count, average, and standard deviation (STDEV) using the following formula in Excel.

  • =COUNT(B2:B51)
  • =AVERAGE(B2:B51)
  • =STDEV(B2:B51)

The results from both sets of data, Diet A and Diet B, have a sample size of 50 (n = 50), which makes it an equal test; this employs the =COUNT(data) function. The mean weight loss is 5.341 kg for group A and 3.710 kg for group B, using the =AVERAGE(data) function. Lastly, the standard deviation for group A is 2.536 kg and for group B, it is 2.769 kg, calculated using =STDEV(data). It noted that the data from group B does have some negative values, which may suggest individuals may have increased their weight.

The mean (=AVERAGE) weight loss is higher in the Diet A group at 5.341 kg, compared to 3.710 kg in the Diet B group, suggesting that Diet A may be more effective.

Examining the standard deviation (SD), Diet A indicates that the SD is closer to the mean (=AVERAGE), while Diet B shows slightly more variation, suggesting a higher SD. This higher SD implies that the data is more spread out, which supports instances of several negative values of -4.148kg and some participants losing over 10.539kg. Based on these figures, it would suggest that Diet A is more effective.

Exercise 6.2:

In this example, the test looked at =MEDIAN(data) and =QUARTILE(data; options).

The =MEDIAN function will return the middle value of the dataset. However, if the dataset consists of an odd set of numbers, the function will select the middle value and then calculate the average of the two middle numbers.

The =QUARTILE function will return the specific quartile based on the percentile.

Finally, the interquartile (IQR) range for Diet A will be calculated by subtracting Q3 from Q1.

From the result, it shows the following

Diet A:

  • Median (=MEDIAN) the middle value: 5.642
  • Q1 (=QUARTILE(data; First Quartile, 25th Percentile)): 3.748
  • Q3 (=QUARTILE(data; Third Quartile, 75th Percentile)): 7.033
  • Interquartile Range (IQR = Q3 - Q1): 3.285

Diet B:

  • Median (=MEDIAN) the middle value: 3.745
  • Q1 (=QUARTILE(data; First Quartile, 25th Percentile)): -4.148
  • Q3 (=QUARTILE(data; Third Quartile, 75th Percentile)): 5.485
  • Interquartile Range (IQR = Q3 - Q1): 9.633

Diet A has a higher median (=MEDIAN) weight loss (5.642) compared to Diet B (3.745), which suggests that Diet A yields better results for weight loss on average compared to Diet B. The first part of the =QUARTILE, Q1 (25th percentile) for Diet A shows that 25% have experienced weight loss (3.748), whereas 25% from Diet B exhibit a negative value (-4.148), indicating that 25% of participants may have either gained weight or lost very little. For the =QUARTILE, the Q3 (75th percentile) section indicates that Diet A (7.033) is higher than Diet B (5.485), suggesting that more participants in Diet A lost weight compared to those in Diet B. The interquartile range (IQR) for Diet A (3.285) is smaller than that of Diet B (9.633). This may suggest that Diet A produces more consistent results for weight loss, as the values are closer together, while Diet B displays more variable results, indicating that weight loss may be inconsistent.

From these findings, it suggests that Diet A is more effective at achieving weight loss, as the results are more consistent. In contrast, Diet B shows variable results, indicating that some individuals lose weight while others do not and may even gain weight. This suggests that Diet B is not as effective compared to Diet A.

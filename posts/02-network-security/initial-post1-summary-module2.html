Summary Post - Digitalisation – What are the security implications of the digital economy?

Collaborative Learning Discussion - Unit 3

Tuesday, 28 November 2023, 12:33 PM

During the initial conversation, the topic focused on the security considerations of the digital economy. One emphasised the remarkable progress made by Homo Sapiens, from our early days as cave dwellers and hunter-gathers to the ground-breaking work of Sam Altman's OpenAI by improving digital intelligence in a manner that will undoubtedly benefit humanity (Allyn, 2023; Brockman & Sutskever, 2015).

As the world of Homo Sapiens continues to evolve rapidly, businesses across various sectors are increasingly turning to the digital sphere to sustain growth and drive future development and profits. However, the drive to remain competitive can sometimes come at a cost. The emphasis on innovation and technology aided by artificial intelligence can result in security being overlooked during the development process, and even user experience can be compromised (Culp, 2022; Wei et al. 2019).

Colleague Kosar, (2023) highlighted that organisations' increasing use of AI has become a tool for cybercriminals to falsify campaigns and lure people into money laundering schemes. As someone with a background in cybercrime, Kosar has witnessed this trend. Kampakis, (2023) also supports this, stating that AI-generated content imitating celebrities has surged and is frequently shared on social media platforms.

The discussion stated that research undertaken by Spremić et al. (2018) revealed that Croatian companies allocated only 1% of their budget to cybersecurity, with the IT department being solely responsible for this area. However, it argued that the research was only conducted with a small, selected number of companies; Finch, (2023) presented a similar study conducted by Moore et al. (2016) done two years earlier in the US suggested that 81% reported that upper management support for cybersecurity, resulting in ample budgets for non-governmental entities. In 2017 alone, the US suffered a consumer loss of $19.4 billion due to cybercrime; in contrast, Croatia is not listed (Petrosyan, 2023).

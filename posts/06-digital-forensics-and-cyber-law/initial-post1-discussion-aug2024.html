Initial Discussion - Cybercrime and Organised Crime

Collaborative Learning Discussion - Unit 2

Monday, 5 August 2024, 10:03 AM

According to Tropina (2010), the development of the digital sphere has profoundly transformed the modus operandi of criminals in conducting illicit activities. The allure of high returns coupled with low risk has rendered it a compelling option for various criminal organisations; furthermore, organised crime groups have the capability to operate outside the jurisdiction of a country's laws, facilitating cybercriminals' ability to target victims in one country while operating in another part of the world. Bernat and Godlove (2012) further support this, asserting that the privacy of the internet poses challenges for law enforcement investigations due to territorial boundaries, which have incentivised traditional criminals to move towards cybercrime.

Kevin Mitnick, a name that reverberates in the global hacking community, was primarily driven by curiosity rather than financial gain when he gained unauthorised access to corporations like Motorola (BBC, 2002; Hesseldahl, 2015). However, the cybercrime landscape is predominantly shaped by financial motivations. These organised cybercrimes, unlike Mitnick's solo exploits, involve the exploitation of vulnerabilities and the collaboration of criminal syndicates. This collaboration can involve thousands of individuals, leveraging the technical expertise of certain members to achieve their criminal objectives (National Crime Agency, 2019; Tropina, 2010).

Efforts to address international crimes present several challenges. Organised crime may operate in countries with limited legislation to combat cybercrime, often due to insufficient funding or a lack of necessary tools, such as technical knowledge (Tropina, 2010). Organisations such as Interpol, with their extensive international reach, play a pivotal role in providing global law enforcement by closely collaborating with public and private sectors to mitigate cybercrime (Interpol, 2017).

According to Deora and Chudasama (2021), cybercrime is becoming prevalent due to most countries' weak regulatory frameworks, the adoption of cybercrime as a business model, and cybercriminals' continuous adoption of new technologies to evade investigation and prosecution. by Mohammed Younes - Saturday, 10 August 2024, 1:21 PM

Dear Amrol

This is an insightful analysis of how the digital sphere has transformed criminal activities, especially in the realm of cybercrime. Tropina (2010) highlighted the challenges posed by the borderless nature of the internet, which complicates law enforcement efforts due to jurisdictional issues. The shift from traditional crime to cybercrime is indeed a significant trend, driven by the low risk and high rewards that the digital environment offers.

However, while the emphasis on financial motivations in cybercrime is well-founded, it's worth noting that the motivations behind cybercrime are increasingly diverse. For instance, hacktivism, where individuals or groups use hacking to promote political ends, is another growing trend (Taylor, Fritsch, & Liederbach, 2014). This adds a layer of complexity to the cybercrime landscape, as the goals of these actors are not solely financial but also ideological.

Moreover, while Interpol and other international organizations play a crucial role in fighting cybercrime, there is an argument to be made that these efforts are sometimes hampered by geopolitical considerations. Countries may be reluctant to fully cooperate in international investigations due to concerns about sovereignty, national security, or political differences (McGuire & Dowling, 2013). This can create gaps in global law enforcement efforts, allowing some cybercriminals to exploit these weaknesses.

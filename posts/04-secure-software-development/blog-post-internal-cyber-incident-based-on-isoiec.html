Internal Cyber Incident based on ISO/IEC

Blog post - Unit 2

Monday, 6 May 2024, 8:53 PM

The International Organization for Standardization/International Electrotechnical Commission (ISO/IEC) (CSRC, n.d.) provides a wealth of guidance and standards for organisations to enhance their security practices through their information security management system (ISMS). This framework equips organisations to actively manage potential security risks and maintain a proactive approach towards their information security (ISO, 2022). The ISO offers detailed documentation that outlines best practices for information security management (ISO, 2019).

The third section of the document, ‘3 Terms and Definitions,’ furnishes a comprehensive list of 77 terms upon which the authorisation of complete document purchases can offer a complete definition. The section serves as a thorough exposition of the ISO requirements that organisations should comply with. The following five terms organisations can follow to prevent internal attacks are as follows.

3.1 - Access Control
Organisations must ensure that access to assets via their systems, such as company logs and data files, is authorised and restricted based on business and security needs. For example, marketing employees should not have access to human resources or financial records.

3.5 – Authentication
Implementing this mechanism ensures that the individual accessing the systems or files is duly authorised, preventing unauthorised personnel from gaining access.

3.14 – Control
Establish controls that can help reduce potential risks. These controls may pertain to implementing processes or devices, including but not limited to computers, which can be instrumental in mitigating unwanted consequences.

3.41 – Management System
The organisation could utilise the management system to address policies and processes.

3.46 – Monitoring
Checking systems and processes is a crucial aspect of ascertaining their operational status regarding any potential critical event.

Additional definitions are available in ISO documentation, but the above points are fundamental commands for preventing possible internal cyber security incidents. Organisations must assess the risks associated with their structure and provide adequate monitoring for their security structure.

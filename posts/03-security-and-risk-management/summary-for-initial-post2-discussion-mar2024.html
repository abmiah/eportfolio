Summary - CVSS

Collaborative Learning Discussion 2 - Unit 7

Tuesday, 9 April 2024, 1:43 PM

The article presents an in-depth exploration of the Common Vulnerability Scoring System (CVSS), a vital tool in the cybersecurity domain. The Forum of Incident Response and Security Teams (FIRST) manages and maintains the CVSS, which is a rating system that assesses known vulnerabilities based on their severity. The significant adoption of the CVSS by esteemed entities such as the US government agency NITS and the MITRE Corporation has made it a staple for cybersecurity professionals. Additionally, the MITRE Corporation provides extensive documentation of vulnerabilities, thus enhancing the value of the CVSS. The CVSS remains a critical tool in effectively identifying and prioritizing vulnerabilities, thereby facilitating improved cybersecurity outcomes (First.org, 2019; Hernández, 2022; Spring et al., 2021).

Spring et al. (2021) raised concerns regarding the dependability and pertinence of a particular system. Aboud (2020) similarly spoke out against the outdated nature of the Common Vulnerability Scoring System (CVSS), which includes threats and vulnerabilities that are not risky. Additionally, Spring et al. (2021) delved into the inadequacies of the formula used to calculate the score, casting doubt on its precision. They also drew attention to the appropriate response level for possible vulnerabilities, as highlighted by my fellow peer Younes (2024).

The Stakeholder-Specific Vulnerability Categorization (SSVC) presents an alternative solution to address the issues surrounding the Common Vulnerability Scoring System (CVSS). Utilising assumptions instead of actual attacks simplifies vulnerability verification. The SSVC framework employs the Attack Tree Diagram, a comprehensive tool for analysing potential system breaches or attacks. While the paper does not delve into the unique characteristics of SSVC, the authors have shared a GitHub repository and encouraged academic contributions, indicating the possibility for further investigation (Spring et al., 2021).

According to my fellow peer Naidoo (2024), the Common Vulnerability Scoring System (CVSS) has proved helpful in determining security measures. However, it is imperative to acknowledge that CVSS should not be the sole criterion for assessing security measures. The SolarWinds attack serves as a case in point. Despite having high ratings, the auditing system was successfully penetrated, highlighting the limitations of CVSS in guaranteeing complete protection against all forms of cyber-attacks. Therefore, it is essential to adopt a more comprehensive approach to cybersecurity and acknowledge the inherent limitations of CVSS. Nonetheless, CVSS remains relevant for assessing risk in cybersecurity.

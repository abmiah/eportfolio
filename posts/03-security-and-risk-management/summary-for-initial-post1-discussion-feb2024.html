Summary - Industry 4.0

Collaborative Learning Discussion - Unit 3

Sunday, 18 February 2024, 8:08 PM

The initial post delves into the inception of the Industrial Revolution, which originated in 18th-century Britain with the advent of steam engines that utilised heated water to generate steam. This resulted in the growth of metropolitan areas and small towns due to the rapid advancement of the Industrial Revolution. However, the modern Industrial Revolution, also known as Industry 4.0 or 4IR, does not entail physical construction or the utilisation of steam engines. 4IR is commonly recognised as the Fourth Industrial Revolution, which includes technologies such as Artificial Intelligence, Machine Learning, and advanced data analytics combined with the Internet of Things (IoT) (Cartwright, 2023; History.com Editors, 2023; McKinsey & Company, 2022; Kovaitė & Stankevičienė, 2019).

Kovaitė and Stankevičienė (2019) conducted a detailed study on the Fourth Industrial Revolution (4IR), highlighting five areas of risk: Technical, Competence, Behavioural, Data Security, and Financial. In a recent post, a ransomware attack on the Royal Mail international delivery system in 2023, where cybercriminals stole data and demanded a hefty ransom of £65.7 million for its return, is discussed (Coker, 2023).

Recognising that an organisation's weaknesses can originate internally, not solely from external sources such as threat actors, is crucial. For instance, Kasar (2024) briefly described that in 2019, Knights Capital Group encountered a massive loss of $440 million within just half an hour due to a computer malfunction that resulted in the sale of all their stocks. This glitch nearly caused the company's insolvency, but investors rescued them by offering $400 million in aid. Although the company labelled the problem as a "Trading Glitch," Heusser (2012) argues that it was a consequence of utilising below-average software development and testing techniques, this would support Kovaitė and Stankevičienė (2019) Technical risk category (Eha, 2012; Popper, 2012).

Younes (2024) suggests a more significant emphasis on competencies-based employee hiring, as highlighted by Kovaitė and Stankevičienė's (2019) risk category. Supporting this view, Hecklau et al. (2016) posit that competencies-based hiring would effectively address the challenges posed by the Fourth Industrial Revolution (4IR).
